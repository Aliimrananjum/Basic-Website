Accessibility

To ensure that this webpage is compatible with a group of diverse users our team had to take great steps to fulfil the resolution. To begin with the team openly discussed the aspect of universal design and inclusive design related to webpage to set a clear understanding for ourselves on what the topic itself means. Our understanding came to this that the goal with UD and ID is to ensure that our webpage is available to as many users possible, regardless of the user’s impediment or hindrance. Therefore, our team listed up disabilities that would make our website less effective with those users with that disability. Doing so we would have a roadmap on how to make our webpage more inclusive.

What have we done?

To ensure that users with color blindless, low vision or even blindness had the opportunity to use our website we had to take some actions. At first, our webpage did not take fully advantage of screen reader, therefore our team ensure that it was possible for a blind user to navigate through the website. Very similar is to be able to tab the keyboard focusable parts for users with motor impairment. To make it simpler to navigate our page we added a “skip to content button” and made image alts as descriptive as possible, even adding the text “menu” to our menu logo.

Then we proceeded to check the colors on our page, settling on a simple color palette of white, black, yellow and blue for our links. We still did contrast checks and testing the page with filter: grayscale (1). We also made our text rather large to make zooming in on a page unneeded, including keeping sans-serif so it is easier to read. Though a problem could be our idea for our front-page image becoming a transparent background when faced with smaller screens or our menu tab focus being transparent. Finally, with users suffering from cognitive impairment in mind we focused on a simple layout and having easy to read text.

After looking at disabilities we took the web page through varies tests to ensure that no problems will incur while using our web page. First off, we checked our CSS with a CSS validator and our HTML with a HTML validator. There we found some small problems, for example background none instead of transparent, hierarchy problems created by <h1>/<h2>/<h3> or what elements were allowed inside a label. After that we proceeded to test the sites on accessibility checker called Eaccesability. our first test showed a miniscule number of problems, but after some editing, we passed it.

Last of all was Achecker, where we only had 2 known problem across the total of pages, one being the use of <i> tag instead of <strong>, the other being that <label> cannot be empty. Since we didn’t have any likely problems, we moved onto potential problems. A lot of them were about the alt in the images and things to make the page a little better, but a lot of them didn’t make sense so we skipped over them. An example is a “skip to content” button, but we already had one. But in went through the checker and added what made sense, like <span lang=””> for the Norwegian mail.

We have also deliberately chosen to not use JavaScript, due to not having it in our curriculum. In order to have more function in our website, we had to find creative solutions to our limitations. This caused some problems for the accessibility of our page. In one case, Achecker promted that the <label> had to contain letters. Due to our design of our navigation bar, we found a workaround to use a unicode that only contained a space. This solution passed the test.
